What makes a good negotiation ?

# Pushing your position without overstating it.
This means pushing your position without overstating it means advocating as forcefully as the facts and law actually support, while resisting the temptation to inflate claims, damages estimates, or threats beyond what you can credibly back up. The reason this matters in negotiation specifically is that the other side often can and will test your claims and once they catch one exaggeration, they start discounting everything else you say, including the parts that were accurate.

# Knowing the facts and law and understanding the strengths and weaknesses of both your position and the other side's.
This means understanding more than a general grasp of the story, it's knowing the documents, timeline, witness statements, and evidentiary record well enough that you're never surprised by something the other side raises. If opposing counsel mentions an email exchange or a clause in a contract, you should already know it exists and what it says, not be hearing about it for the first time across the table. 

# Distinguishing stated positions from underlying interests
This means looking beyond what people say they want (their positions) to understand why they want it (their underlying interests). A position is a specific demand or solution, while an interest reflects the needs, concerns, values, or motivations driving that demand. By identifying underlying interests, negotiators can uncover common ground, generate creative alternatives, and reach solutions that satisfy the core needs of all parties rather than becoming stuck in disagreements over fixed positions.

# Knowing when to make the first offer, how to sequence concessions, and how to avoid giving things away for nothing.
This means knowing when to make the first offer, planning concessions carefully, and ensuring every compromise is exchanged for something of equal value. Making the first offer can help shape the discussion when you have good market knowledge. Concessions should be gradual and strategic, showing flexibility without weakening your position. Most importantly, never give something away for free, ask for a concession in return to maintain balance and strengthen the overall agreement.

# Switching between collaborative (interest-based) and competitive (positional) tactics depending on the counterpart and stakes.
This means adapting your approach based on both the relationship and the stakes. When trust is important or long-term cooperation matters, you learn to collaborate by focusing on shared interests, open questions, and value creation. When stakes are high, information is asymmetric, or the counterpart is highly competitive, you shift to a positional style, protecting your key demands and making firmer, more bounded concessions. The skill lies in reading the situation early and fluidly moving between the two modes so you can build value when possible but defend your position when necessary.

# keeping expectations realistic and getting authority to move before you're at the table.
This means keeping expectations realistic in negotiation means grounding your targets in data, constraints, and likely trade-offs rather than ideal outcomes, so you avoid anchoring too high or committing to positions you can’t justify. At the same time, getting authority to move before you reach the table is about internal alignment knowing your walk-away limits, approved concessions, and decision boundaries in advance. This prevents hesitation or “I need to check” moments that weaken your credibility and gives you the confidence to respond decisively while still staying within defined parameters.
